Pigovian tax

A Pigovian tax is a tax on any market activity that generates negative externalities.

Fat tax is a kind of Pigovian tax, so before we talk about fat tax, we need to know what is Pigovian tax to make better sense about fat tax later.

A Pigovian tax is a tax on any market activity that generates negative externalities.

Externality means the costs not included in the market price. In other words, it affects a third party who did not expect to have that cost.

The diagram illustrates the working of a Pigovian tax. A tax shifts the marginal private cost curve up by the amount of the externality.

Fat tax

A fat tax is a tax or surcharge that is placed upon fattening food, beverages or on overweight individuals.

Fat tax is a practical example of Pigovian tax. It is a tax or surcharge that is placed upon fattening food, beverages or on overweight individuals.

A fat tax aims to discourage unhealthy diets and offset the economic costs of obesity, which is the externality we want to use it to reduce. It can reduce the consumption of foods that are high in saturated fat, such as fast food.

Mechanism

Numerous studies suggest that as the price of a food decreases, individuals get fatter.

Numerous studies suggest that as the price of a food decreases, individuals get fatter. In fact, eating behavior may be more responsive to price increases than to nutritional education. Estimates suggest that a 1 cent per ounce tax on sugar-sweetened beverages may reduce the consumption of those beverages by 25%.

Problems

The mechanism may not work as expected.

However, there can also be some problems.

The first problem is that the mechanism may not work as expected.

Although people will buy less food as the price of it increases, there is also evidence that obese individuals are less responsive to changes in the price of food than normal-weight individuals, which means the obese individuals, the targeted people, contribute less to the purpose of diminishing obesity.

It may be because to obese individuals, as their demand of food is larger, the price elasticity of demand is smaller.

What products should be targeted?

Another problem is about what products fat tax should target. This must be done with care, because a carelessly chosen food tax can have surprising and perverse effects.

For instance, saturated fat and salt are substitutes. Consumption patterns suggest that taxing saturated fat would induce consumers to increase their salt intake, thereby putting themselves at greater risk for cardiovascular death.

Another reason for us to choose food carefully is that there are in fact not much studies about what food has strong association with obesity. Current proposals frequently single out sugar-sweetened drinks as a target for taxation. Cross-sectional, prospective, and experimental studies have found an association between obesity and the consumption of sugar-sweetened drinks. However, experimental studies have not always found an association, and the size of the effect can be very modest.

Fat tax can be burdensome for the poor.

Since the poor spend a greater proportion of their income on food, a fat tax might be regressive. Taxing foods that provide primarily calories, with little other nutritional value reduces this problem, since calories are readily available from many sources in diet of industrialized nations.

To make a fat tax less burdensome for the poor, proponents recommend earmarking the revenues to subsidize healthy foods and health education. Additionally, proponents have argued that the fat tax is less regressive to the extent that it lowers medical expenditures and expenditures on the targeted foods among the poor.

Indeed, there is a higher incidence of diet-related illnesses among the poor than in the general population.
